# Pitfall: Commodity Pricing Power Fallacy

**Assuming rising ASP (Average Selling Price) indicates raw pricing power (ability to raise prices on identical parts), rather than product mix shift (customers buying higher-spec parts).**

## Symptom
The agent assumes a hardware or passive component supplier (e.g., Murata, TDK, Ibiden) is aggressively raising prices on legacy/identical parts by 15%-35% to drive margins, and models a high risk of customer margin backlash or rapid collapse when supply catches up. In reality, the company's ASP is rising purely because customers are migrating to higher-specification, higher-value components (e.g., high-capacitance/high-voltage parts for AI racks, ultra-low-loss substrate layers).

## Why
In hardware/passive component cycles:
1. **Identical Parts Deflation:** Real-world semiconductor/passive component buyers expect deflation on identical legacy parts over time. Blanket price hikes on identical legacy items trigger customer resentment, driving active secondary sourcing qualification.
2. **Value-Added Mix Shift:** True structural growth is driven by the physical architecture of next-gen AI hardware (e.g., Blackwell/Rubin power densities requiring miniaturized, high-capacitance/high-voltage capacitors). Customers willingly pay 2×–3× ASP premiums for these upgraded components because they represent genuine performance improvements, not opportunistic gouging.

## How to avoid
1. **Analyze ASP Drivers:** When evaluating a company's revenue expansion, interrogate whether the ASP increase is driven by price hikes on identical products or structural product mix shift (miniaturization, higher capacitance, advanced materials).
2. **Assess Sustainability:** Shortage-driven price gouging on legacy parts is temporary and collapses when capacity expands. Mix-shift driven ASP expansion is far more sticky and sustainable because the underlying product requirements are hardcoded into the customer's next-generation bills of materials (BoM).
3. **Verify via Primary Sources:** Cross-reference general analyst reports ("price hikes") with primary company IR/Summit slides that break down revenue by volume, mix shift, and pure pricing.
